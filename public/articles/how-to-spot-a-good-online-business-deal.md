# How to Spot a Good Online Business Deal

**Category:** Buying Businesses | **Tags:** Online Business · [evaluate a business before buying](/article/how-to-evaluate-a-business-before-buying-it) · Beginner Guides · Guides & How-To's · [Best Passive Income Investments for Beginners](/article/best-passive-income-investments-for-beginners)

**Target Keywords:** how to spot a good online business deal, buying an online business, online business due diligence, evaluate website for sale

---

## Summary

The online business marketplace is full of listings that look incredible on the surface—steady monthly profits, diversified traffic, clean financials, and a seller who claims they're only selling because they want to "pursue other opportunities." The reality is that some of those listings are exactly what they appear to be: solid, cash-flowing businesses that will run well under new ownership. But many others are dressed-up problems. Inflated traffic numbers, revenue spikes manufactured right before listing, over-reliance on a single Google ranking or Amazon account, and financials that don't hold up under scrutiny are far more common than most first-time buyers expect. Spotting the difference between a genuinely great deal and an expensive mistake requires knowing exactly what to look for—and what questions to ask before you hand over a dollar. This guide walks you through the complete framework for evaluating an online business listing: the financial metrics that matter, the traffic signals you need to verify, the red flags that should stop a deal in its tracks, and the green flags that separate a real opportunity from a polished pitch deck.

---

## Why Buying an Online Business Is Different From Buying a Local Business

When you buy a brick-and-mortar business—a dry cleaner, a restaurant, a landscaping company—you can walk through the door. You can see the equipment, talk to the employees, watch the customers come in, and get a feel for the operation. The business exists in physical space with physical evidence.

Online businesses don't work that way. The assets are intangible: traffic that lives in Google Analytics, revenue that flows through Stripe or Amazon Seller Central, content that ranks because of backlinks you can't see or fully control. The "location" of an online business is its Google ranking, its social following, its email list, or its app store position—and all of those can change overnight with no warning.

This makes online business due diligence simultaneously simpler and more dangerous than its offline equivalent. Simpler because most of the key data is digital and can be verified with the right tools and access. More dangerous because sellers know exactly which screenshots to cherry-pick, which months to highlight, and which metrics to leave off the listing page.

In 2024, the US saw 9,546 closed business transactions totaling approximately $7.59 billion in enterprise value—a 15% increase over 2023—according to data from [Acquire.com vs Flippa](/article/acquire-com-vs-flippa-which-is-better-for-buyers). More buyers than ever are entering the online business market. That increased demand means listings are priced more aggressively, and the margin for error in your due diligence has narrowed.

Understanding how to evaluate what you're actually buying—not what the listing page says you're buying—is the most important skill you can develop as an online business investor.

## Step 1: Understand What Type of Business You're Evaluating

Before you dig into any specific metrics, you need to understand the business model. Each type of online business has different risk profiles, different valuation frameworks, and different things that can go wrong. The due diligence process for a SaaS product looks almost nothing like the process for a content affiliate site.

### The Main Categories of Online Businesses for Sale in 2025

**Content / Affiliate Sites**
Revenue comes from display advertising (Mediavine, AdThrive/Raptive, Google AdSense) and/or affiliate commissions (Amazon Associates, ShareASale, etc.). The business lives or dies on organic search traffic. These sites are the most vulnerable to Google algorithm changes—and 2023–2024 saw some of the most destructive algorithm updates in search history (more on this below).

- Typical valuation: 25×–34× monthly net profit for top-quality sites; lower for sites with traffic instability
- Key risk: Google algorithm dependency
- Key asset: Content library, backlink profile, topical authority

**E-Commerce / Shopify Stores**
Revenue comes from selling physical or digital products directly to consumers. These businesses require inventory management, supplier relationships, and customer service infrastructure.

- Typical valuation: 2.5×–4× annual SDE (Seller's Discretionary Earnings) for smaller stores
- Key risk: Platform dependency (Shopify, Amazon), supply chain, customer acquisition cost
- Key asset: Brand, customer list, supplier relationships

**Amazon FBA (Fulfilled by Amazon)**
A subset of e-commerce where inventory is stored and shipped by Amazon. These businesses are entirely dependent on Amazon's platform, rules, and algorithm.

- Typical valuation: 3×–5× annual EBITDA
- Key risk: Amazon account suspension risk, competition from Amazon's own products, platform dependency
- Key asset: Product listings, reviews, BSR ranking

**SaaS (Software as a Service)**
Revenue comes from subscription software. These businesses have the most predictable revenue of any online business type and command the highest multiples.

- Typical valuation: 3×–10× annual recurring revenue (ARR) for private companies, depending on growth and churn
- Key risk: Technical debt, churn rate, single developer dependency
- Key asset: Code base, customer contracts, MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)

**Service-Based Online Businesses**
Agencies, consulting firms, freelance operations. Revenue comes from client work. These are highly owner-dependent and often difficult to transfer.

- Typical valuation: 2×–3× annual SDE
- Key risk: Owner dependency, client concentration, contract renewal
- Key asset: Client relationships, team, SOPs

Understanding which category you're in tells you where to focus your scrutiny. A content site needs deep traffic analysis. A SaaS business needs technical code review and churn analysis. An e-commerce store needs supplier verification and inventory auditing.

## Step 2: Master the Valuation Basics — What Are You Actually Paying For?

Online businesses are almost universally valued as a multiple of earnings, not of revenue. Understanding the difference between the main earnings metrics—and how multiples work—is the starting point for evaluating whether a price is fair.

### Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)

SDE is the standard earnings metric for online businesses under $10 million in value. It represents the total financial benefit available to a full-time owner-operator, calculated as:

**SDE = Net Profit + Owner's Salary/Draw + Non-Cash Expenses + One-Time/Non-Recurring Expenses + Personal Expenses Run Through the Business**

The add-backs are where sellers can get creative. Pay close attention to what has been added back and verify that each add-back is legitimate. Common legitimate add-backs include the owner's salary (since a new owner would either replace it with their own or hire someone), depreciation, and genuinely one-time expenses like legal fees from a resolved dispute.

Common problematic add-backs include inflated "one-time" expenses that actually recur, personal expenses that were questionable business deductions, and revenue that has already dried up.

### EBITDA

Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Used for businesses valued above approximately $10 million. For most individual buyers looking at [Flippa vs Empire Flippers](/article/flippa-vs-empire-flippers), Empire Flippers, Acquire.com, or Motion Invest, SDE is the relevant metric.

### How Multiples Work

Once you have a clean SDE number, the business is valued at a multiple of that number:

**Business Value = Clean SDE × Industry/Quality Multiple**

As of 2024–2025, typical multiples by business type and size (based on data from Flippa, FE International, and BizBuySell) are:

| Business Type | Typical SDE/EBITDA Multiple | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Content/Affiliate Site | 25–34× monthly (≈ 2–3× annual) | Top quality; lower for volatile traffic |
| E-Commerce / Shopify | 2.5–4× annual SDE | Stabilized at ~3.98× by late 2024 |
| Amazon FBA | 3–5× annual EBITDA | Reflects platform concentration risk |
| SaaS (small, private) | 3–10× ARR | Varies widely by churn and growth rate |
| Service / Agency | 2–3× annual SDE | Owner-dependency penalty common |

**Deal size also matters significantly.** Flippa's 2024–2025 data shows that median profit multiples step up with deal size: 1.68× for $10K–$100K deals, rising to 2.43× for deals over $1 million. Larger businesses present stronger systems, documented SOPs, and broader buyer competition—all of which support higher pricing.

The key question when evaluating a multiple is: **does this multiple make sense given this specific business's risk profile?** A content site with diversified traffic, a strong backlink profile, 36 months of stable earnings history, and low owner time commitment might justify a 30× monthly multiple. The same revenue from a site that gets 80% of its traffic from one Google keyword, with 12 months of earnings history and a seller who works 40+ hours a week on it, should be priced at a significant discount.

## Step 3: Verify the Financials — Trust But Verify (Mostly Just Verify)

Financial verification is the heart of due diligence. Every number the seller presents needs to be confirmed against primary sources, not just accepted from a PDF or screenshot.

### The Documents You Should Request

For any online business acquisition, request all of the following before making an offer:

1. **Profit & Loss (P&L) statements** — at minimum 24 months of monthly P&L; ideally 36 months
2. **Bank statements** — 12–24 months, verified against the P&L revenue figures
3. **Tax returns** — 2–3 years; compare reported revenue to tax filings (discrepancies are a major red flag)
4. **Platform earnings reports** — Amazon Seller Central, Stripe, PayPal, Shopify, Google AdSense/AdThrive dashboards, affiliate network dashboards
5. **Google Analytics access** — direct read-only access to the account, not screenshots
6. **Google Search Console access** — to verify traffic and keyword ranking history
7. **Expense documentation** — contracts with contractors, software subscriptions, advertising spend

**A critical warning from Empire Flippers:** "If initially they share the information in unofficial forms like screenshots, then make sure you get into the dashboards and financial documents to confirm the accuracy of the figures." Screenshots are trivially easy to manipulate. Insist on live dashboard access during a screen-share call whenever possible.

### The Financial Red Flags That Should Stop a Deal

The following patterns, identified consistently by business brokers and acquisition specialists, should at minimum prompt deep investigation and at maximum prompt you to walk away:

- **A sudden revenue spike in the 3–6 months before listing.** A business that was generating $3,000/month for two years and suddenly jumped to $6,000/month right before listing is suspicious. Ask for a detailed explanation. Was there a viral moment? A new product launch? A new traffic channel? Or did the seller pump paid ads to inflate earnings they knew would be used to set the asking price? According to DealStream, revenue spikes with "no clear driver (e.g., new product launch, viral campaign)" are a classic manipulation tactic.
- **Revenue concentrated in a single source.** If 70%+ of revenue comes from one affiliate program, one Amazon listing, one advertising network, or one client, the business is far more fragile than the headline numbers suggest.
- **Financials that don't reconcile with tax returns.** According to the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants, financial record discrepancies are identified in more than 40% of small business acquisitions. If the P&L shows $200,000 in annual revenue but the tax return shows $140,000, someone is lying to someone—and it might be you they're lying to.
- **Missing months or selective data sharing.** Any seller who can only provide 6 months of data, or who provides full data for some platforms but not others, is almost certainly hiding something.
- **Unusually high RPM (Revenue Per 1,000 Visits) for a content site.** When RPM seems significantly above niche benchmarks, verify every monetization source independently.
- **Expenses that seem too low.** Some sellers run a content site on $200/month in expenses while claiming $8,000/month in revenue. That's possible, but verify every cost line. Are there contractors who aren't being disclosed? Advertising spend that's being hidden? Tools that weren't counted?

## Step 4: Audit the Traffic — The Life Blood of Any Online Business

For content sites, e-commerce stores, and any business dependent on organic or paid traffic, traffic analysis is as important as financial analysis. Traffic is to an online business what foot traffic is to a retail store—and it can be manufactured, manipulated, or structurally fragile in ways that aren't visible in a revenue screenshot.

### Request Direct Google Analytics Access

Never accept screenshots. Never accept exported CSV files that the seller prepared. Demand direct read-only access to the Google Analytics account.

Once inside, look at:

1. **Traffic trend over 24–36 months** — Is the trend stable, growing, or declining? A site with flat or declining traffic that's still generating revenue is a business in decay, not a business at its peak.
2. **Traffic sources breakdown** — What percentage of traffic comes from organic search vs. direct vs. social vs. referral vs. paid? Heavy dependence on a single channel (especially paid, which disappears the moment the ad budget stops) is a risk factor.
3. **Geographic distribution** — US traffic is more valuable for ad-revenue sites than traffic from India or Southeast Asia. A site with 60% US traffic will monetize very differently than one with 60% traffic from lower-CPM markets.
4. **Bounce rate and session duration** — High bounce rates (70%+) combined with very short sessions suggest traffic that isn't actually engaging with the content—sometimes a sign of bot or low-quality traffic.
5. **Traffic source authenticity** — Look for unusual spikes in direct traffic (a classic sign of bot traffic being routed through direct to clean up suspicious source data), inconsistent geographic patterns, or traffic from data center IP ranges.

### The Google Algorithm Risk — The Biggest Blind Spot for Content Site Buyers

If you're buying a content or affiliate site in 2024–2025, you cannot ignore what Google's algorithm updates have done to the content site market.

Google's March 2024 Helpful Content Update (HCU)—incorporated into its core ranking algorithm on March 5, 2024, and completing a 45-day rollout on April 26, 2024—was one of the most destructive updates in search history for niche content publishers. Google stated the update would "reduce low-quality, unoriginal content in search results by 40%."

The data from independent studies confirmed the destruction:

- A study of 7,105 niche/link-building sites found the **average Monthly Organic Traffic (MOT) decrease was 82.12%**, with a **median decrease of 97.37%** (Paul Teitelman SEO)
- 27% of affected sites lost 91–100% of their organic traffic
- 22% of sites lost all traffic entirely
- Travel affiliate publishers were among the hardest hit: 32% of the 671 travel sites analyzed lost more than 90% of their organic traffic (Boomcycle Digital Marketing)
- For sites that have seen "recovery," most SEO professionals report only about one-third of original traffic returning—and that's considered a win
- Flippa's H1 2025 data reported a **37% decline in content site sales volume** tied directly to Google's algorithm changes

This is not historical trivia. This is the operating environment for anyone buying a content site right now. Any content site you're evaluating was either impacted by these updates or survived them. Finding out which is your first priority.

**What to check for content site traffic:**

- Request Google Search Console data going back 24+ months and look for traffic drops in September 2023 (HCU), March 2024 (HCU incorporated into core), and August 2024 (core update). Any unexplained drops during these windows should be investigated thoroughly.
- Check Ahrefs or SEMrush to see organic keyword ranking history. A site that shows stable traffic in Google Analytics but lost 60% of its keyword rankings 18 months ago is a business running on borrowed time.
- Verify traffic diversification: does the site have an email list? Social following? Direct traffic from brand recognition? Sites with diversified traffic sources survived the HCU far better than pure-SEO plays.

### Checking Traffic Quality With Third-Party Tools

Even if you have direct Google Analytics access, use independent tools to cross-verify:

- **Ahrefs or SEMrush** — Check organic keyword rankings, backlink profile, and traffic estimates. Compare third-party traffic estimates to what the seller claims. Large discrepancies are a warning sign.
- **SimilarWeb** — Provides traffic channel breakdown and geographic distribution as an independent data point.
- **Wayback Machine** — Review what the site looked like 1–3 years ago. Suddenly upgraded sites that look polished but have thin content history might be recent flip attempts.
- **WHOIS history** — Check domain ownership history. Has the domain changed hands multiple times? Serial flippers who buy, polish, and re-sell businesses are not inherently bad, but rapid ownership changes in a short window are worth investigating.

## Step 5: Assess the Risk Profile — What Could Blow This Up?

Every online business has concentration risks. The question is whether those risks are manageable and priced appropriately into the deal, or whether they're existential threats disguised by clean monthly profit numbers.

### The Concentration Risks Every Buyer Must Evaluate

**Platform dependency**
Is the business entirely dependent on one platform that can change its rules, algorithm, or payment terms at any time?

- An Amazon FBA business where 90% of revenue comes from one ASIN is vulnerable to a single bad review campaign, a competitor with a better listing, or Amazon deciding to sell the same product directly.
- A content site where 85% of traffic comes from Google is at risk every time Google pushes a core update.
- A Shopify store that relies on Meta Ads for 80% of customer acquisition will see its economics evaporate if ad costs increase or its ad account is suspended.

As a general rule: **no single platform, customer, or traffic channel should account for more than 30%–40% of revenue.** Businesses that exceed this threshold should be priced at a discount that reflects the concentration risk.

**Customer concentration**
For service businesses and B2B models, check whether any single client accounts for more than 20% of revenue. According to DueDilio, a healthy business has a diverse customer base where no single account makes up more than 20% of revenue—anything above this represents substantial risk. If you [buy a small local business](/article/how-to-buy-a-small-local-business) and that client leaves in month three, you've bought yourself a very expensive problem.

**Owner dependency**
This is the silent killer of many online business acquisitions. If the business only works because of the current owner's relationships, reputation, technical knowledge, or personal brand, then what you're buying isn't really a business—it's a job that comes with a price tag.

Ask the seller directly: How many hours per week do they work in the business? What would happen if they disappeared tomorrow? Are there documented Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for all key tasks? Is the team capable of running the business without the seller's direct involvement?

**Flippa's due diligence guide notes:** "If the owner is heavily involved in daily operations or key relationships, that's a problem. This means that the business might not function well without them. Look for signs of systems, processes, and team independence."

**Backlink profile quality**
For any content site, run a full backlink audit using Ahrefs or Moz before closing. Private Blog Networks (PBNs), spammy foreign-language backlinks, or a sudden spike in link acquisition right before listing are all signals that the seller may have artificially inflated their Google rankings in a way that could collapse post-acquisition—or worse, trigger a Google manual penalty that destroys traffic entirely.

**Legal and compliance risks**
Outstanding lawsuits, unpaid taxes, unresolved IP disputes, and regulatory violations don't disappear when the ownership changes—they transfer to you. Request a full legal disclosure and have a business attorney review any agreements, contracts, customer terms of service, and pending disputes before closing.

## Step 6: Identify the Green Flags — What Makes a Deal Worth Pursuing

Great deals do exist. Here's what separates a genuinely attractive online business from one that's merely well-presented.

### 7 Green Flags That Signal a High-Quality Online Business

**1. Long, stable earnings history (24–36+ months)**
Not months 1–12. Not the last 6. At least 24 months of consistent or growing monthly revenue that shows the business can sustain itself through normal market cycles. Bonus: the earnings held steady or grew through the 2023–2024 Google algorithm updates.

**2. Traffic diversification across multiple sources**
Organic search AND email AND social AND direct. A business with 40% of traffic from Google, 25% from email, 20% from direct, and 15% from social has a far more resilient revenue base than one that's 90% Google dependent. FE International identifies traffic diversification as one of the core drivers of a higher valuation multiple.

**3. Revenue diversification across multiple streams**
Content site with both display ads AND affiliate commissions AND a digital product. E-commerce store that sells on its own Shopify site AND on Amazon AND has an email list of repeat buyers. The more revenue streams, the less vulnerable the business is to any single channel shutting off.

**4. Low owner time commitment (under 10–15 hours/week)**
A business that requires less than 10–15 hours per week of owner time—and can demonstrate this with documented SOPs, a reliable contractor network, or an employed team—is far more transferable and will command premium multiples. Every hour of required owner time is a dollar of value reduction.

**5. Clean, auditable financials with no discrepancies**
Three years of tax returns that reconcile with P&L statements. Platform dashboards that match the reported revenue. Expenses that are fully documented and logical. A seller who provides clean financials without resistance is a seller who has nothing to hide.

**6. A logical, credible reason for selling**
The best sellers are retiring, moving on to a new venture, or selling to free up capital for a larger opportunity. These motivations don't require the business to be performing poorly. Be more skeptical of sellers who claim they "just don't have time anymore" for a business that generates $15,000/month—that rationale doesn't hold up to scrutiny.

**7. Transferable assets that don't depend on the seller personally**
The domain is clean and fully owned. The brand has genuine goodwill (reviews, loyal customers, social following) that will transfer. The supplier and affiliate relationships are documented and contractual, not personal handshakes. The content is legally owned and not scraped or licensed from a third party.

## Step 7: Run the Numbers — A Simple Framework for Evaluating Deal Quality

Before making an offer on any online business, run this quick framework to pressure-test the economics.

### The DAD Investor Quick-Evaluation Framework

**D — Does the cash flow cover the debt service?**

If you're financing the acquisition, the business must generate enough cash flow to cover your loan payments and still leave you with meaningful take-home income. Use the DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) formula:

> **DSCR = Annual Clean SDE ÷ Annual Debt Service**

A DSCR of at least 1.25× is the standard lender requirement. A DSCR of 1.5× or higher gives you meaningful cushion for unexpected revenue dips in the transition period.

*Example:* If a business has $72,000 in annual clean SDE and your [How to Finance Buying a Small Business](/article/how-to-finance-buying-a-small-business) payment is $48,000/year, your DSCR is 1.5×—comfortable. If the SDE is $55,000 and your loan payment is $48,000, your DSCR is only 1.15×—too tight.

**A — Are the assets transferable and verifiable?**

Walk through every major asset and confirm it can be transferred to a new owner:

- Domain name: registered to the seller with no liens; age and authority are real
- Google Analytics account: historical data transfers with the account
- Social media accounts: accessible, not tied to personal profiles that can't be separated
- Supplier agreements: in writing, not verbal relationships the seller maintains personally
- Affiliate program access: confirm with affiliate networks that the account can be transferred (many programs have transfer restrictions)
- Email list: verify subscriber count and open rate; a 100,000-subscriber list with 3% open rate is worth far less than 15,000 subscribers with 28% open rate

**D — Does the deal price reflect the actual risk?**

After completing your due diligence, ask yourself: what are the top three risks to this business, and does the asking price appropriately discount for those risks?

- Heavily Google-dependent content site: discount the multiple versus a diversified site
- Amazon FBA with one dominant ASIN: discount the multiple versus a diversified product catalog
- Service business with owner-dependent client relationships: discount the multiple versus a systemized team-based operation
- Business with 12 months of earnings history: discount the multiple versus one with 36+ months

## What to Do When You Find a Deal You Like

Once you've worked through the framework above and remain interested in a listing, here's the sequence of next steps before signing anything:

1. **Sign an NDA.** Any seller worth working with will require this before sharing detailed financials. It protects both of you.
2. **Request all financial documentation** listed in Step 3. Compare everything against primary sources.
3. **Get direct access** to Google Analytics and Google Search Console. Schedule a screen-share call to review live data together.
4. **Run independent SEO tools** (Ahrefs, SEMrush) to cross-verify traffic claims and backlink quality.
5. **Talk to the seller directly.** Ask how they built it, why they're selling, what's been hardest, and what they'd do differently. Genuine sellers talk about their business with genuine knowledge. Evasive or scripted answers are a warning sign.
6. **Make a conditional LOI (Letter of Intent).** This outlines the deal terms and price, subject to satisfactory completion of due diligence.
7. **Hire a business attorney** to review all contracts, including the asset purchase agreement, any non-compete clauses, and representations and warranties.
8. **Negotiate the transition support period.** Ask for at least 30–90 days of seller support post-closing where the seller remains available to help with questions and introductions.

## 10 Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

To summarize the warning signs covered throughout this article:

1. **Seller refuses to provide direct dashboard access** — only sends screenshots
2. **Revenue spike in the 3–6 months immediately before listing** with no clear explanation
3. **Financials don't reconcile with tax returns** — different numbers reported in different places
4. **70%+ of traffic from a single Google keyword or page** — one algorithm update away from collapse
5. **Single customer, product, or platform accounting for 50%+ of revenue**
6. **No documented SOPs** and a seller who works 40+ hours/week in the business
7. **Backlink profile full of PBNs, spammy links, or sudden link spikes** before listing
8. **Content site traffic that didn't hold through the 2023–2024 Google HCU updates**
9. **Seller is evasive about reason for selling** or gives implausible answers under gentle follow-up
10. **Financials are missing months** or only cover a suspiciously favorable time window

## Marketplace Quick Reference: Where to Buy and What to Expect

- **Empire Flippers** — Curated marketplace; 90% rejection rate on listings. All financials are pre-verified. Best for buyers with $100K+ budgets who want vetting and migration support. Average sale time approximately 48 days.
- **Flippa** — Open marketplace with 2M+ registered buyers. Broader range of deal sizes, including entry-level listings under $10,000. Buyers bear more of the due diligence burden. Best for experienced buyers or those buying smaller deals as a first acquisition.
- **Acquire.com** — SaaS-focused marketplace. Good source of early-stage or micro-SaaS opportunities. Buyers verify their own funds; seller verification is lighter than Empire Flippers.
- **FE International** — Full-service broker for deals $500K+. Provides its own due diligence reports and has handled more than 1,500 transactions. Higher fees, but significant hand-holding throughout the process.
- **Motion Invest** — Focused on content sites under $500K. Known for thorough vetting and hands-on migration support. A good option for first-time content site buyers.

## Key Takeaways

- Online businesses are valued as a multiple of clean Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)—verify that the SDE is accurate before trusting any multiple
- Content and affiliate sites were severely impacted by Google's 2023–2024 Helpful Content Updates; always verify traffic stability through at least the March 2024 update
- Never accept screenshots in place of direct dashboard access to Google Analytics, Google Search Console, and platform earnings dashboards
- Revenue concentration (one customer, one product, one traffic source) is the single most common risk that buyers underestimate in online deals
- The green flags that justify premium pricing: 24–36+ months of stable earnings, diversified traffic and revenue, low owner time commitment, and clean transferable assets
- The DSCR formula (Annual SDE ÷ Annual Debt Service) must produce at least 1.25× for a financed deal to be serviceable
- Build in a 30–90 day seller transition support period in every deal—this is non-negotiable for a smooth handoff
- On any deal over $50,000, hire a business attorney and CPA who understand digital asset acquisitions before you sign anything
